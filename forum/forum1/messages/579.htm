Josie, it sounds as if the Brazilian doctor was set to "con" the boy's grandparents into having him "done" strictly for the money the doctor expected to get for the procedure. It seems to me you would be well advised to take the boy to your NIH practitioner who in all likelihood will find nothing whatever wrong with him, which should set his mind at rest. It's been some years since I last visited Brazil, but based on my observations there very few Brazilians are circumcised, unless they've recently undergone an orgy of it in response to the HIV-AIDS epidemic. (One of my Brazilian hosts most ungraciously joshed me about being circumcised ['Who cut you?'] at the swimming pool even before I'd got my clothes off. He was right, of course, but it was the first time I'd found circumcision considered a valid source of humor.) I'm more horrified by needlessly pulling teeth than by the ill-advised effort to rush into circumcision. I hope the teeth pulled were not permanent teeth. One thing I noted in Brazil was that many (if not most) otherwise attractive young people have a number of permanent teeth missing. I laid this to the excessive sugar consumption common in Brazil, but it didn't cross my mind that it could be a custom there to pull healthy teeth. . . I hope you don't have to send the kid back there; it sounds like a country hazardous to health! Best wishes, RJK.
